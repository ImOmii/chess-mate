Published November, 2017

It’s the first week back at university for the new semester and it’s time to get feedback on our MVP from our module tutor and mentor. Walking into the meeting we did not expect many positive points or a positive grade for the MVP, as we had technical difficulties. However the grade that we received will not be reflected on our final grade.

To our surprise, our feedback was better than expected. As we did in fact have a working MVP but was unable to demo it during the presentation, this was enough to warrant us a pass as well as how we handled the situation where the technical error happened. We remained calm and got through as best as we could, trying to fill the void left by the technical fault.

This uplifted our mood drastically and motivated us to resume our work on the game, most importantly assured us to not make the same mistake and come back with the best possible game we can produce. We regrouped for our second meeting of the new semester enthusiastic to start planning for the improvements on our game. Before we could start making improvements, there is quality assurance report that is to be submitted along with an individual reflection report on the first semester and the MVP. The quality assurance report is a technical report answering the following question: “How will you ensure that your software is high quality?”

Given the size of the report, it was split between a few of us in the group. Divided into sections between the members who worked on it then one member accumulated the information, properly structured and then submitted.

The first hurdle we had was a memory leak in the system which was really problematic and caused the computer to crash. A memory leak is a scenario that occurs when objects are no longer being used by the application, but the Garbage Collector is unable to remove them from working memory. As a result, the application consumes more and more resources – which eventually leads to a fatal OutOfMemoryError. In our case it was to do with LibGDX, the sprites kept getting re-rendered onto the page.

Our games logic was designed simply for users to play against other users. Given our plan to implement an AI, the games logic needed re-writing so that the logic was compatible with an AI and it was highly coupled; the methods were overloaded with tasks and returning results that didn’t make sense nor were needed. An AI is necessary so that the game can have a single player mode, naturally the user would not play against their self and this is where the AI comes in.
